10-Minute Clarity Sprint

The unexamined life isn't a philosophy problem. It's a ten-minute problem.

Socrates said the unexamined life is not worth living.

He meant it as a practice, not a profession.

This is the practice.

Ten minutes of writing that surfaces what you're already carrying — before it quietly makes the decision for you.

The thing you keep circling is already deciding. You just don't know what it's deciding yet.

I spent six months trying to name what I was building. Couldn't get there by thinking about it. Then I wrote a story — and the thing I'd been circling showed up in the second paragraph.

Your brain can't lie in a story the way it can in a plan. The analytical mind performs. The story reveals.

Every person who has gone through this says the same thing afterward:

"I didn't plan to write that."

That's the point.

Set a timer. Follow the steps in order. Don't skip ahead.

What's the thing you keep coming back to?

Not a goal. Not a plan. The thing that's been taking up space — a decision that won't resolve, an idea that won't leave, a question you keep turning over without landing anywhere. The rougher it is, the more honest the story tends to be.

I keep coming back to...

Give it a name.

Three to five words. The version of this where it's already resolved. Not a tagline — a shorthand for what you'd call it on the other side.

When this resolves, it's...

One question before you write.

It changes what the story needs to surface.

Four questions. Three minutes.

Answer based on what you planned — not what you hope the story shows.

The person who needs this most — their job, situation, and what they're struggling with.

Word of mouth? A post they read? A problem they Google? How does the right person find their way to you?

Not for you — for them. What's different about their life or work after working with you?

The alternative they'd settle for — another tool, another person, doing nothing, figuring it out alone.

How long do you want to write?

10 minutes is enough to surface something real. 15 gives it more room. Either way — don't stop writing.

Write your answers down. Your unguided answers are more honest.

Who's in your story — and who isn't?

One person wrote an entire future about his business and never mentioned a single customer — only the work itself. He hadn't realized he'd been building for the craft, not the market. Another wrote a rich picture of her life and never once put her partner in it. Who was there? Who was absent? What does the gap tell you?

What did you assume stayed the same?

One person wrote a detailed story about her consulting practice and assumed the client relationships she has now would still exist in 5 years. She hadn't noticed she'd written zero new business development into the future. She was building toward a version of success that required her current relationships to last forever. What did you assume stays the same that might actually change?

What didn't you write — and what does that mean?

One person's idea was about building a community. He wrote extensively about the content and the platform — and never once wrote about what it felt like to be in the room with the people he was building for. He hadn't noticed he was building infrastructure for a community he'd never actually imagined. What was conspicuously missing? What did you not let yourself write?
